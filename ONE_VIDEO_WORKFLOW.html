What moves through the workflow

The raw recording stays as the source. The automatic edit is only a proposal. The final reviewed output comes after a person checks and corrects that proposal.

Step by step

The six stages

These stages describe what happens to a single raw recording. Batch processing repeats this same process for many videos.

Prepare the recording

The system separates the audio from the video, reduces steady background noise, and finds where speech and long pauses occur.

  • Protects the original recording.
  • Makes speech easier to understand.
  • Finds pauses that may be shortened.

Create reviewable text

Spoken Croatian is turned into timed text, then split into sentence-sized pieces that can be reviewed next to the video.

  • Shows what was said.
  • Knows when each sentence happened.
  • Gives reviewers manageable pieces.

Find likely mistakes

The system searches for repeated takes, false starts, stutters, fragments, side comments, long silence, and audio disruptions. AI helps spot them, but fixed rules make the actual cut decisions.

  • Uses both sound and transcript evidence.
  • When something was said twice, the later take always wins — a simple rule the professor can rely on while recording.
  • Risky or uncertain suggestions become flagged spots for the reviewer, not automatic cuts.

Render the automatic first draft

The proposed edit is used to create an automatically edited video. This is useful for review, but it is not treated as final.

  • Uses the original video picture.
  • Uses cleaned audio.
  • Adds small fades around cut points.

Review and correct

A reviewer opens the review tool: the video and its transcript side by side, with every proposed cut marked. Each suggestion comes with a plain-language explanation of why it was made and how confident the system is.

  • Restore useful explanations with one click.
  • Cut bad takes that were missed.
  • Check natural pacing and lesson continuity.
  • Produce the final video from the same screen.

Render the final reviewed output

The reviewed decision set becomes the final reviewed video for the next production step.

  • Keeps automatic and reviewed decisions separate.
  • Preserves a path back to the raw recording.
  • Hands off a video that has human approval.

Safety model

What gets saved along the way

The workflow is safer because each important layer is saved separately. A later mistake can be inspected instead of guessed.

The preserved layers

Original raw recording The untouched source. This is the recovery point.
Automatic analysis Transcript, timing, silence detection, and attention markers.
Automatic edit plan The system's first proposal for what to keep and cut.
Reviewed edit plan The human-corrected decisions, saved separately from the automatic plan.
Final reviewed video The output produced from the reviewed decisions.

Why this matters

The process is designed to support correction. If the automatic edit cuts too much, the reviewer can restore it. If someone later asks why a section was removed, the team can compare the raw recording, the automatic proposal, and the human-reviewed decision.

  • The raw recording is not overwritten.
  • The automatic first draft is not the final authority.
  • The reviewed output can be traced back to decisions.
  • The team can rerun or improve the process without losing the source.

Human review

What the reviewer is checking

The reviewer does not need to understand the technical pipeline. Every suggested cut explains itself in plain language, uncertain spots are highlighted, and the reviewer only needs to verify that the final lesson still makes sense and sounds natural.

Keep the lesson intact

  • Was a useful explanation cut? Restore it.
  • Was an incorrect take kept? Cut it.
  • Did a correction get handled properly? Keep the correct version.
  • Does the math or explanation still flow? Check before approving.

Keep the video natural

  • Is a pause too short? Adjust the decision if it feels rushed.
  • Does a transition sound awkward? Review the nearby cut.
  • Is a short filler phrase actually natural? It can stay.
  • Is the system uncertain? Inspect the highlighted area.

Predictable by design

Why the AI is not the editor

Early versions let the AI make more of the editorial choices. We measured those choices against real human editors and replaced the ones a simple rule does better — so the system behaves the same way every time.

Fixed rules make the cuts

  • Repeated something? The later take is always the one that stays. The professor can flub, pause, and simply say it again — the earlier attempt is cut, every time.
  • Short phrases like "Dobro." or "Ok."? Never cut automatically, even when they repeat — they are natural speech, not mistakes.
  • AI wants to remove a unique explanation? That never happens automatically. It becomes a flagged suggestion the reviewer decides on.
  • Every AI suggestion is verified against the actual recording before it can become a cut.

Measured against real editors

  • The benchmark is human work: 98 lessons that human editors already edited, compared word by word.
  • The keep-the-later-take rule comes from that data: it matches what human editors chose in the large majority of cases — better than the AI's own judgment did.
  • Disagreements are visible, not hidden: a comparison view shows, on the same transcript, what the system cut versus what the human editor cut.
  • Every change to the system is tested against this benchmark before it ships, so quality moves in one direction.
  • Same video in, same edit out: predictable rules mean results the team can trust and reproduce.

Production boundary

Where this process ends

The AI video editor prepares and reviews the edited video. Gradivo still owns the final publishing workflow.

AI video editor owns

  • Temporary media needed for processing.
  • Speech analysis and editing transcript.
  • Automatic edit decisions and attention markers.
  • Human-reviewed decisions.
  • Rendering the reviewed output.

Gradivo still owns

  • Which raw recording belongs to which problem.
  • Production status and responsibility tracking.
  • Upload to the final video platform.
  • Student-facing transcript, chapters, and explanations.
  • Final review, publishability, and publishing.

The editing transcript is not the final student transcript

The transcript created during AI editing helps the system make and review cut decisions. Gradivo should still create the student-facing transcript from the final reviewed video, so the transcript matches exactly what students watch.

Core principle: AI suggests, predictable rules decide, and a human confirms the final lesson.
